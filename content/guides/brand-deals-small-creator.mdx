# How to Negotiate Brand Deals as a Small Creator

The follower count threshold for landing brand deals is lower than most creators think. Brands pay for engaged, targeted audiences. A 5,000-follower account with a passionate fitness niche and 12% engagement rate is more valuable to a supplement company than a 200,000-follower general entertainment account with 1.5% engagement.

This guide covers the full process: what makes a small creator attractive to brands, how to price your content, how to build a media kit, and how to write outreach that gets responses.

## Why small creators get hired

Brands have two distinct budget categories. Large campaign budgets go to macro influencers (500K+ followers) for brand awareness. Smaller, performance-driven budgets go to micro influencers (1K–50K followers) for conversion.

The logic: a micro influencer's audience trusts their recommendations at a higher rate because the relationship feels personal, not transactional. Community-reported conversion data consistently shows micro influencer campaigns delivering higher click-through and purchase rates per dollar spent than macro campaigns.

For brands selling to a specific demographic, a 10,000-follower account with a highly targeted audience is more efficient than a 1M-follower account with a diffuse one.

**What brands actually look for in small creators:**

- Engagement rate above 5% (comments, saves, shares weighted more than likes)
- Content-audience alignment (your audience matches their buyer profile)
- Consistent posting schedule (reliability signals professionalism)
- Content quality (clean audio, clear visuals, structured delivery)
- Prior sponsored content that was disclosed properly and performed

If you have these, you are brand-deal ready regardless of follower count. For a full comparison of how brand deal income stacks up against Creator Rewards at different follower counts, see [Creator Rewards vs brand deals](/guides/creativity-program-vs-brand-deals).

## How to price your content

There is no universal rate card, but there are consistent frameworks.

**Base formula:** Average video views × $0.01–$0.03

A creator averaging 8,000 views per video could reasonably quote:
- Low end: 8,000 × $0.01 = $80 per video
- High end: 8,000 × $0.03 = $240 per video

The range reflects niche, engagement quality, and deliverable complexity. Finance, health, and software niches command the higher end. Entertainment and lifestyle sit at the lower end.

**Add-ons that increase rate legitimately:**

| Add-on | Typical additional cost | Why brands pay it |
|---|---|---|
| Usage rights (30 days) | +25--50% of base rate | Brand can repurpose your video in paid ads |
| Usage rights (90 days) | +50--100% of base rate | Longer campaign use without renegotiation |
| Exclusivity | +20--50% of base rate | Creator won't promote competitors during a set period |
| Whitelist/boosting | Negotiated separately | Brand runs paid ads through your handle |
| Multiple deliverables | Each additional video discounted 10--20% | Package pricing for volume |
| Story or carousel addition | +$50--150 | Additional format coverage |

**What not to accept:**

- "Exposure only" deals from brands with real marketing budgets
- Product gifting without cash when the product value is below your base rate
- Contracts requiring unlimited revisions without a defined cap
- Exclusivity clauses lasting more than 30–60 days without a premium

A useful reference point: if a brand runs paid ads, they typically pay $20–$100+ per 1,000 impressions (CPM). Your content creates impressions at a fraction of that cost. Price accordingly.

## Build your media kit

A media kit is a one-to-two page document that gives a brand everything they need to decide whether to work with you. Think of it as a professional summary, not a sales pitch.

**What to include:**

**Creator bio (2–3 sentences).** Who you are, what content you make, who your audience is. No fluff. "I'm a fitness creator focused on beginner strength training for people over 40. My audience is primarily women 35–55 in the US and Canada who are new to lifting."

**Audience demographics.** Screenshot from TikTok analytics: age breakdown, gender split, top countries. This is the data brands actually buy.

**Key performance metrics.** Average views per video (last 30 days), average engagement rate, follower count, monthly reach if available.

**Content categories and sample work.** 3–5 links to your strongest sponsored or niche-relevant videos. Link to your TikTok profile.

**Rate card.** One video, package of 3, with and without usage rights. Keep it scannable.

**Contact info.** Email is fine. Do not send brands to a DM.

Keep the visual design clean. A PDF built in Canva with your brand colors takes 2 hours to make and raises your perceived professionalism immediately. Update it monthly or whenever metrics meaningfully change.

## Finding brands to pitch

Most small creators wait to be discovered. The ones who build brand deal income actively create inbound and outbound pipelines.

**Inbound signals:**
- Add "business inquiries: [email]" to your TikTok bio
- Include a media kit link (Google Drive PDF works)
- Post content that is clearly brand-friendly: clean, structured, professional tone

**Outbound outreach targets for small creators:**

Start with brands that already spend money on creator content. Three categories that consistently work with micro influencers:

1. **DTC (direct-to-consumer) brands.** Supplement companies, skincare brands, software tools, home goods, food and beverage. These brands run influencer campaigns as their primary customer acquisition channel.

2. **Software and apps.** SaaS companies with free trials or freemium models actively use creators for awareness. The product is digital, so there is no inventory risk. Many have formal ambassador or affiliate programs.

3. **Brands you already use.** Authenticity is a real selling point. If you genuinely use a product and can talk about it naturally, your pitch is credible in a way that generic outreach is not.

**Where to find contacts:**

- The brand's Instagram or TikTok: look for "partnerships" or "collaborations" in bio
- LinkedIn: search "influencer marketing manager" + brand name
- Brand website: marketing or press contact email
- Creator marketplaces: TikTok Creator Marketplace (access at 10K followers), Grin, AspireIQ, Collabstr

## Outreach that gets responses

Most brand outreach emails fail because they lead with the creator's follower count instead of the brand's opportunity.

**Structure that works:**

**Subject line:** Keep it specific. "Collaboration idea for [Brand Name] + [Your Niche]" outperforms "Partnership inquiry" every time.

**Opening line:** Reference something specific about the brand. Not a generic compliment. "I noticed you ran a campaign with fitness creators last month" shows you did 30 seconds of research.

**The pitch (2–3 sentences):** Who your audience is, why they are relevant to the brand, and what you propose. Be specific about the deliverable.

**Social proof:** One metric or result. "My last product review video averaged 14,000 views and had 340 saves." Data closes faster than adjectives.

**Clear ask:** A call to action that requires a yes/no. "Would you be open to a quick call this week?" or "I've attached my media kit — happy to answer any questions."

**Sample outreach email:**

> Subject: Fitness Creator Partnership Idea — [Brand Name]
>
> Hi [Name],
>
> I'm a fitness creator on TikTok focused on beginner strength training for women over 40. My audience is 78% women, ages 35–55, primarily in the US.
>
> I've been using [Product] for three months and think it would resonate strongly with my audience. My recent gear review averaged 11,000 views with a 9% engagement rate.
>
> I'd love to propose a 2-video package covering [specific use case]. I've attached my media kit with full audience demographics and rates.
>
> Would you be open to a quick call this week?
>
> [Name] | [TikTok handle] | [Email]

That email is 120 words. It takes 60 seconds to read and answers every question a brand manager has before they respond.

## Negotiating rates

Most brands come back with a counter. This is normal. A few principles:

**Know your floor.** Before you pitch, decide the minimum you will accept for a given deliverable. Knowing your floor prevents you from agreeing to undervalue in the moment.

**Counter with justification.** If a brand offers below your rate, counter with a specific reason tied to audience value, not effort. "My audience demographic matches your buyer profile closely, which is reflected in my engagement rate" is stronger than "I work really hard on my content."

**Bundle instead of discounting.** Instead of lowering your per-video rate, offer a bundle: two videos at a slight discount. You earn more total; they get more coverage. Both parties win.

**Be willing to walk.** Brands that push rates below fair value often have other problematic contract behaviors. One well-paid deal beats three undervalued ones.

## What to put in a contract

Do not work without a written agreement. A simple contract covers:

- Deliverables: how many videos, what format, what platform
- Timeline: post date, revision rounds, approval process
- Payment terms: amount, payment method, due date (net 30 is standard)
- Usage rights: what the brand can do with your content, for how long
- FTC disclosure requirement: #ad, #sponsored, or TikTok's branded content toggle
- Kill fee: if the brand cancels after you've started work, you keep a portion

Docusign and HelloSign handle e-signatures. For deals under $500, a PDF contract works fine. For larger deals, consider having an attorney review the template once.

## After the deal: setting up the next one

The easiest brand deal to close is the second one with a brand that already worked with you.

Deliver the content on time. Share performance data after posting: views, engagement rate, comment sentiment. Send a brief recap email: "The video hit 9,400 views in 7 days with a 10.5% engagement rate. Happy to discuss a follow-up campaign."

Brands measure creator ROI. If you make their reporting easy, you become the low-friction option for future campaigns.

Keep a simple spreadsheet of every brand you've contacted, the outcome, and the date. Brands change marketing managers. A pitch that didn't land in March may land in September with a new contact.
