# TikTok Analytics: Which Metrics Actually Drive Revenue (2026)

<TableOfContents />

Most creators check the wrong numbers. Views feel like progress, and a video blowing up to 500K plays feels like a win — until your Creator Rewards dashboard shows $30 in earnings. That gap happens because views don't pay you. Qualified views do. And qualified views depend on metrics most creators aren't watching.

This guide covers the numbers that actually predict revenue, where to find them in TikTok Studio, what good and bad look like, and a 10-minute weekly routine to keep your content moving in the right direction.

<CalloutBox type="info" title="The short version">
  <ul>
    <li>Completion rate is the #1 signal for both algorithm distribution and RPM.</li>
    <li>Qualified view rate predicts what you'll actually get paid for.</li>
    <li>Audience geography can change your per-view earnings by up to 15x.</li>
    <li>Follower count and likes are vanity metrics. They tell you almost nothing about revenue potential.</li>
  </ul>
</CalloutBox>

## The metrics that actually drive revenue

### Completion rate

Completion rate is the percentage of your video the average viewer watches. It's the single most important metric in the Creator Rewards ecosystem.

Here's why it matters so much: TikTok uses completion rate as its primary quality signal at every distribution checkpoint. The current threshold for meaningful FYP expansion is around 70% completion (up from ~50% in 2024). Videos under 40% completion get suppressed even if the overall engagement looks decent.

It also feeds directly into earnings. Videos retaining viewers past 30 seconds show 40–60% higher RPMs than low-retention content, according to creator-reported data. Completion above 80% can double RPM compared to content sitting at 40–50%.

**Where to find it:** TikTok Studio > Analytics > Content > select any video > Video performance tab.

**Benchmarks:**

| Completion rate | What it means | Distribution outcome |
|---|---|---|
| 80%+ | Excellent | Wide FYP push, viral distribution likely |
| 60–79% | Strong | Moderate expansion |
| 40–59% | Average | Limited FYP reach |
| Below 40% | Poor | Actively suppressed |

These are community-reported figures. TikTok doesn't publish official threshold numbers.

**What to do about it:** If your completion rate is under 50%, the problem is almost always the hook or the first 15 seconds. See [how to increase watch time on TikTok](/guides/increase-watch-time-tiktok) for a detailed retention breakdown.

### Qualified view rate

Your qualified view rate is the percentage of total views that count toward Creator Rewards earnings. A view only qualifies when it hits 5+ seconds of watch time, originates from the FYP or TikTok Search (as of December 2025), and comes from a unique viewer who didn't tap "Not Interested."

Most creators with well-performing content see 40–60% of total views qualify. Low-retention content can drop below 20%, meaning 80% of your views are generating zero revenue.

**Where to find it:** TikTok Studio > Creator Rewards Program > select a video > Qualified Views column.

Watch the ratio, not just the raw number. A video with 50,000 total views and a 60% qualification rate earns more than a video with 200,000 total views and a 12% qualification rate.

### Audience geography

Where your viewers are located affects your RPM more than almost any other variable. TikTok's RPM reflects advertiser demand in the viewer's country, not where you live.

Creator-reported data from 2025 shows the spread:

| Market | RPM range |
|---|---|
| US-dominant audience | $0.80 – $2.00+ per 1,000 qualified views |
| United Kingdom | $0.60 – $1.20 |
| Germany / France | $0.40 – $0.90 |
| Brazil / Mexico | $0.15 – $0.40 |
| Eastern Europe | $0.13 – $0.30 |

The US vs. developing market gap can hit 15x. Two creators with the same view count, same completion rate, and same posting frequency can earn wildly different amounts because of geography alone. This is also the most common cause of unexpected low earnings when a video goes unexpectedly viral outside a creator's usual audience.

**Where to find it:** TikTok Studio > Analytics > Followers > Follower territories. Also check individual video performance for traffic source breakdowns.

### Saves and shares ratio

In TikTok's rewards formula, saves and shares carry more weight than likes. A like signals passive enjoyment. A save signals "I need this later" — a strong indicator of genuine value that TikTok rewards.

Track saves and shares as a rate: saves per 1,000 views and shares per 1,000 views. If your content is consistently generating both, you're building the engagement signal that pushes RPM higher through the Additional Reward bonus.

**Where to find it:** TikTok Studio > Analytics > Content > select any video > Interactions.

## Vanity metrics vs. revenue metrics

Most creators spend their mental energy on the wrong column:

| Metric | Why it feels important | Why it's misleading |
|---|---|---|
| Total views (legacy) | Big numbers feel like success | TikTok now shows Engaged Views as the default. Total views included swipe-pasts and don't predict earnings. |
| Engaged Views (new default) | Feels more accurate than total views | Better than total views but still not the same as qualified views. Engaged Views counts viewers who watched past a swipe, but doesn't account for traffic source, geography, or the 5-second qualified threshold. Treat it as a middle indicator, not a revenue predictor. |
| Likes | Easy to count, emotionally satisfying | Low weight in rewards formula; less meaningful than saves |
| Follower count | Milestone-based thinking | You only need 10K for Creator Rewards; more followers don't raise RPM |
| Following feed views | Still a "view" on the counter | Doesn't count as a qualified view, earns nothing |

| Revenue metric | What it actually tells you |
|---|---|
| Completion rate | Quality of your hook and content structure |
| Qualified view rate | Percentage of views generating revenue |
| FYP + Search traffic % | What share of your views can become qualified views |
| Audience Tier 1 % | What share of viewers are from high-RPM markets |
| Saves per 1,000 views | Engagement quality signal for Additional Reward |

A video with 50,000 views, 75% completion, and 55% qualification from a US audience will almost always out-earn a video with 500,000 views, 15% completion, and 8% qualification from a mixed global audience. The math isn't close.

## Reading your TikTok Studio dashboard

TikTok Studio (available at studio.tiktok.com or through the app: Profile > three-line menu > Creator Tools > TikTok Studio) is where all analytics live. Here's where to find the metrics that matter:

**Overview tab:** Gives you aggregate data across a date range. Useful for trend-spotting but not for video-level analysis. TikTok now shows Engaged Views as the default metric here instead of total views. Engaged Views filters out swipe-pasts, which makes it more useful than the old total views number, but it still doesn't tell you about qualified views or earnings. Don't spend much time here.

**Content tab:** This is where you work. Select any video to see individual performance data including completion rate, average watch time, traffic source breakdown, and audience retention graph. The retention graph shows you exactly where viewers are dropping off — which tells you whether your problem is the hook, the middle, or the payoff.

**Creator Rewards tab:** Shows qualified views by video, RPM estimates, and earnings. Cross-reference this with your Content tab data to understand which content variables correlate with higher qualified view rates.

**Follower tab:** Shows audience demographics including geography, age, and active hours. The geography breakdown here is how you know whether your audience is in high-RPM or low-RPM markets.

**Analytics checkpoints to review per video:**
1. Traffic source breakdown — what percentage came from FYP vs. profile visits, search, or following feed
2. Retention graph — where exactly viewers drop off
3. Completion rate vs. your average
4. Qualified views as a percentage of Engaged Views (or total views if your dashboard still shows the legacy metric)

## The 10-minute weekly analytics routine

Pick one day per week. Every video from the previous week gets a quick audit.

**Minutes 1–3: Completion rate scan**
Check completion rate on every video posted in the last 7 days. Flag any under 40% — those are not getting meaningful FYP distribution. Note the highest-performing video for analysis.

**Minutes 3–5: Traffic source check**
For your top video, check what percentage of views came from the FYP and Search combined. If that total is under 60%, you're leaking views into sources that can't generate qualified views (Following feed, profile visits). This may indicate your content isn't reaching new audiences.

**Minutes 5–7: Qualified view rate**
Open the Creator Rewards tab. Compare qualified views to Engaged Views for each video. If your ratio is dropping week over week, something changed in your hook quality or posting behavior. Note: the qualified-to-Engaged-Views ratio will be higher than the old qualified-to-total-views ratio since Engaged Views already filters out swipe-pasts.

**Minutes 7–9: Geography check**
Open the Follower territory breakdown monthly. If your audience is shifting toward lower-RPM markets, or if a video went viral in a geography outside your core, that's the explanation for earnings that feel low relative to views.

**Minutes 9–10: Identify one variable to test**
Based on what you saw, pick one thing to change or test next week. Not five things — one. Maybe your hooks need to be faster. Maybe you need to cut videos at the 90-second mark instead of 2 minutes. Testing one variable at a time gives you data you can actually use.

## Common analytics mistakes

**Checking daily.** Analytics data is noisy in the first 24–48 hours. A video that looks like it bombed on day one can pick up steam on day three. Weekly review gives you signal instead of noise.

**Obsessing over follower count.** You need 10,000 followers to apply for Creator Rewards. Beyond that, follower count doesn't raise your RPM or your qualified view rate. A creator with 15K highly engaged, US-based followers can earn more than one with 200K global followers who are largely passive.

**Ignoring geographic data.** This is the most expensive mistake. If half your audience is from markets where TikTok RPM is $0.15 per 1,000 views, your effective RPM will reflect that no matter how well your content performs.

**Confusing Engaged Views with qualified views.** TikTok now defaults to Engaged Views instead of total views, which is an improvement, but Engaged Views is still not the same as qualified views. Engaged Views tells you people watched past a swipe. Qualified views tells you those watches counted toward earnings. If you're not checking the Creator Rewards tab for qualified view data specifically, you're missing the metric that drives revenue.

**Ignoring traffic source mix.** Your follower count grows your Following feed traffic as you scale. That traffic doesn't count toward qualified views. FYP and Search views both qualify, but Following feed and profile visit traffic does not. Watch the combined FYP + Search percentage and make sure it stays dominant.
